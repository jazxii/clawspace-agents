WCAG 3 is not a compliance target yet. It will not be one for years. So why are enterprise procurement teams starting to ask about its five-level scoring model?

Because the framing has already shifted, even if the standard has not landed.

The March 2026 WCAG 3 working draft confirms the direction. Outcome-based scoring across five levels, replacing the binary pass/fail of WCAG 2.x. Expanded scope to native mobile and AI-generated content. Documented testing methodology required for any conformance claim.

Three things I am noticing in 2026 procurement language.

One. RFPs are starting to ask for "accessibility maturity level" rather than "WCAG 2.1 AA conformance." The maturity framing is downstream of the WCAG 3 scoring direction. Buyers want a number, not a binary.

Two. VPATs are getting longer. Some vendors are now including a methodology section that describes how testing was done, not just the result. This is exactly what WCAG 3 will require formally. Smart vendors are pre-staging.

Three. AI-generated UI tools that ship a "WCAG report" with a violation count are starting to look thin compared to competitors that score across categories. The scoring framing punishes tools that hide behind a low number.

What this means for product teams that ship AI accessibility tooling.

If your dashboard reports 12 violations, it is reporting WCAG 2.x style. That will keep working through 2027 and 2028. After that, "12 violations across 4 components scoring 2.4 out of 5 on perceivable" is going to be the comparison your buyers expect.

If you build internal accessibility scorecards, this is a good year to start scoring across categories. Perceivable, operable, understandable, robust. Five levels each. Not because WCAG 3 demands it yet, but because the language has already moved.

What I am not saying. APCA. Time-to-completion. Any of the specific WCAG 3 outcome metrics that may not survive the next draft. The structure of the scoring is what is hardening. The specific metrics are still in flux.
