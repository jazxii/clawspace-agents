# WCAG 3 scoring — already shaping procurement

**Format**: 7-slide carousel. Cover, the shift, three signals, what to do, the takeaway.

---

## Caption (front-loaded)

WCAG 3 is not a compliance target yet. It will not be one for years.

So why are enterprise procurement teams already asking about its five-level scoring model?

Because the framing has already shifted, even when the standard has not landed.

Swipe for three things I am seeing in 2026 procurement docs and what to do about them.

Save this for your next vendor review.

## Slide 2: The shift

**Headline:** From binary to scored

**Body:**
March 2026 WCAG 3 working draft confirms the direction.
Outcome-based scoring across five levels.
Expanded scope to native mobile and AI-generated content.
Documented testing methodology required for any conformance claim.
**Image prompt:** "A two-column comparison. Left column titled 'WCAG 2.x' shows a single pass/fail icon. Right column titled 'WCAG 3' shows a five-level scoring chart. Minimal flat infographic, accessible blue-purple palette, no people."

---

## Slide 3: Signal one — RFP language

**Headline:** RFPs ask differently

**Body:**
"Accessibility maturity level" is replacing "WCAG 2.1 AA conformance" in procurement docs.
The maturity framing is downstream of WCAG 3 scoring.
Buyers want a number, not a binary.
**Image prompt:** "A document titled 'RFP — Accessibility' with one phrase highlighted in yellow: 'accessibility maturity level'. Below the highlight, a small handwritten 'not pass/fail' note. Clean editorial layout, accessible palette, no people."

---

## Slide 4: Signal two — VPATs

**Headline:** VPATs are getting longer

**Body:**
Smart vendors are adding methodology sections.
"Here is how we tested" is the new requirement.
This is exactly what WCAG 3 will require formally. Pre-staging works.
**Image prompt:** "A stacked stack of three VPAT-style documents. Each one is taller than the last. The top doc has a 'methodology' chapter highlighted. Minimal flat illustration, accessible blue-purple palette, no people."

---

## Slide 5: Signal three — AI scorecards

**Headline:** Violation counts are aging

**Body:**
"12 violations" reads thin against "12 violations across 4 components, 2.4 out of 5 on perceivable."
Tools that score across categories make tools that hide behind a low number look weak.
**Image prompt:** "Two scorecards side by side. Left card just says '12 violations' in plain type. Right card shows the same number but broken out into four POUR categories with a five-level score on each. Minimal flat illustration, accessible palette, no people."

---

## Slide 6: What to do

**Headline:** Score across categories

**Body:**
If you build internal a11y scorecards, this is the year to score across POUR.
Perceivable. Operable. Understandable. Robust.
Five levels each.
Not because WCAG 3 demands it yet. Because the language has already moved.
**Image prompt:** "A four-row scorecard with rows labeled Perceivable, Operable, Understandable, Robust. Each row has a five-segment score bar with one segment highlighted. Clean infographic, accessible blue-purple gradient, no people."

---

## Slide 7: The takeaway

**Headline:** Structure is hardening, metrics are not

**Body:**
The structure of scoring is what is hardening in WCAG 3.
The specific metrics (APCA, time-to-completion) are still in flux.
Build for the structure now. Wait on the metrics.

Has WCAG 3 framing shown up in your procurement docs yet?

**Image prompt:** "A bold pull-quote card. 'Structure is hardening. Metrics are still in flux.' Cream background, deep purple accent, editorial layout, no people, accessible contrast."
