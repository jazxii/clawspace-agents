## Caption (front-loaded)

Most of the AI-and-a11y discourse is about what LLMs break.

A February 2026 paper looked at the other side. arXiv 2502.18701.

Researchers built a browser plugin that uses a generative AI model to restructure webpage HTML in real time. Heading hierarchy correction. Content labeling. E-commerce pages were the test bed.

They ran it through automated accessibility testing. Then they put it in front of blind and low-vision users.

The finding. Revised webpages "offer significant improvements over the original webpages regarding screen reader navigation experience."

Automated tools have measured "improved heading structure" before. Users telling researchers the navigation actually got better is the part that is rare.

Why this matters.

We have spent a decade asking authors to ship accessible HTML. A view-time repair layer does not need the original author to cooperate.

The 30 year old government PDF rendered as broken HTML. The vendor checkout flow you cannot edit. The internal tool nobody owns. A repair layer reaches all three.

The risks are real. Hallucinated headings. Made up labels. A model deciding a clearance banner is decorative. None of those are hypothetical.

But the direction is worth watching. This is the first credible plan B I have seen that does not depend on overlays.
