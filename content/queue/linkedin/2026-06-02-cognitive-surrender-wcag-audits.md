Came across a term last night that sharpened how I think about AI in accessibility work: cognitive surrender.

It is from Shaw and Nave's 2026 Wharton paper on AI and human reasoning, surfaced in Addy Osmani's latest essay. Offloading is handing off the work but still owning the answer. Surrender is when the AI's output quietly becomes your answer, with nothing left to check. Across 1,372 participants, when the AI was wrong, people accepted it 73% of the time.

For Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) auditing, that line is everything. Scanners top out near 57%. The cognitive-flow layer, screen reader output, and ARIA judgment are invisible to them. No agent signs off compliance. A person does. Human in the loop is still the bar.

That is not an argument against AI. Most of the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) software development life cycle (SDLC) is predetermined process: triage, evidence capture, regression sweeps, documentation. Rote work eating hours that should go to judgment. Offload the process. Never surrender the verdict.

Done right, ADA engineers stop burning days on boilerplate and spend them on calls only a human can make.

If you run an ADA workflow, which steps are you letting AI accelerate, and where does the human verdict stay non-negotiable?
