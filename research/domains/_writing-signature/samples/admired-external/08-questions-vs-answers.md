Want your team to take accessibility seriously? Stop asking questions. Start giving answers.

"Is this ADA compliant?" "Is this accessible?" "Did anyone check the contrast?"

Those questions feel like advocacy. They're not. They are a way to surface a concern without taking on the work of explaining what's actually wrong, and most teams will quietly file the question under "we'll come back to that" because there's no clear next step.

The version that moves things forward sounds different:

"This button doesn't work with a keyboard. There's no visible focus indicator, and pressing Enter doesn't trigger the click handler. WCAG 2.1.1 covers this. Without it, anyone using a keyboard - or assistive tech that operates through one - can't complete the form. Here's how I'd fix it."

One of those is a question. The other is a contribution.

If you're the only person on your team who cares about accessibility, asking questions makes you the gatekeeper - the person whose job it is to find what's broken. Giving answers makes you the teammate who notices and fixes things. That's a different role, and people respond to it differently.

You don't have to know everything to start. Pick one issue you can fully explain. Read the relevant WCAG criterion. Try the page with a keyboard, a screen reader, or both. Bring the issue with the explanation already attached.

Then do that again the next time. The skill compounds.
