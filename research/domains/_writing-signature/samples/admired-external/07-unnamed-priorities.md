Most accessibility debates aren't really about accessibility. They're about unnamed priorities.

When a team argues about whether to fix a low-impact bug or build a new feature, the real question isn't "is this accessible enough?" - it's "what are we optimizing for?"

Compliance is one answer. Pass the audit. Avoid the lawsuit. Hit the WCAG threshold. Done.

User experience is a different answer. Make sure people can actually use the thing. Test with screen readers. Talk to disabled users. Iterate on the friction.

Speed of shipping is another answer. Ship the feature, fix what gets reported. Accept that some users will hit barriers in the meantime.

These aren't all the same goal. They produce different roadmaps. They produce different decisions.

The honest version of the conversation is: "We're optimizing for compliance this quarter. Fixes that don't affect our audit score get deprioritized." Or: "We're optimizing for our power users. Most of them use a screen reader, so accessibility regressions are P0." Or: "We're optimizing for time-to-market on this launch. We'll come back and audit the new features in Q3."

Each of those is a defensible choice. None of them is the only choice. What gets a team in trouble is making decisions that imply one of them while saying out loud that they're optimizing for something else.

The fastest way to make better accessibility decisions isn't to learn more about WCAG. It's to be honest about what you're actually trying to achieve.

What are you optimizing for? And does the team you work with agree?
